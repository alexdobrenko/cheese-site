The Canon

Twelve cheeses you should be able to identify blindfolded

Not the full encyclopedia. Just the ones that, if you know them well, unlock every cheese plate, wine pairing, and grocery store decision you'll ever face.

Parmigiano-Reggiano
Emilia-Romagna, Italy
The king. Aged 24-36 months. Crystalline crunch, umami depth. If you're grating the pre-shredded stuff from a green can, we need to talk.
Hard
Comté
Jura, France
Tastes like toasted hazelnuts and brown butter. France's most-produced cheese and somehow still underrated outside of France. Every wheel is different.
Hard
Brie de Meaux
Île-de-France, France
Not the rubbery supermarket stuff. Real Brie de Meaux at room temperature is a near-liquid, mushroomy, earthy experience. Eat the rind.
Soft
Roquefort
Aveyron, France
Sheep's milk blue, aged in limestone caves. Sharp, salty, a little aggressive. The blue cheese that makes people understand blue cheese.
Blue
Gruyère
Fribourg, Switzerland
The perfect melting cheese. Fondue wouldn't exist without it. Nutty when young, almost caramel-like when aged. The backbone of French onion soup.
Hard
Manchego
La Mancha, Spain
Sheep's milk, firm, with a distinctive herringbone rind. Serve it with membrillo (quince paste) and you've got the easiest impressive appetizer on earth.
Semi-hard
Mozzarella di Bufala
Campania, Italy
Made from water buffalo milk. Tears when you pull it apart and releases a milky liquid. Eat it within hours of making it. The further from Campania, the sadder it gets.
Fresh
Époisses
Burgundy, France
Washed in brandy. Smells like it could clear a room. Tastes like salted caramel and earth. Banned from French public transport. Enough said.
Soft
Cheddar
Somerset, England
Real cheddar, cloth-bound, from Somerset. Sharp, crumbly, with crystals. Has almost nothing in common with the orange blocks most people picture. Completely different food.
Hard
Gorgonzola Dolce
Lombardy, Italy
The gentle blue. Creamy, sweet, spreadable. If someone says they don't like blue cheese, start them here. With honey and walnuts, it's a dessert.
Blue
Pecorino Romano
Lazio, Italy
The salty one. Sheep's milk, sharp, essential for cacio e pepe and carbonara. People who use only parmesan in pasta are missing half the picture.
Hard
Camembert de Normandie
Normandy, France
Brie's earthier, punchier cousin. Bake it in its box. Pour honey on top. Tear bread and dip. One of the best things you can do in under 20 minutes.
Soft

Opinions

Things that are true about cheese

  1. Cheese should be at room temperature. Taking cheese from fridge to mouth is a crime against flavor. 45 minutes minimum.
  2. The rind is usually edible and usually good. Wax and cloth, no. Bloomy white rinds, washed rinds, natural rinds, yes.
  3. Expensive cheese is almost always worth it. A $22 wedge of aged Comté will bring more joy than $22 of almost anything else at the grocery store.
  4. Pre-shredded cheese contains cellulose (wood pulp) to prevent clumping. It melts weird. Grate your own.
  5. American cheese is good, actually. On a smash burger, on a breakfast sandwich. Knowing this doesn't make you less of a cheese person. It makes you more of one.
  6. Cheese plates need exactly three things: something hard, something soft, something blue. Fruit and nuts if you want. Crackers are a vehicle, not a feature.
  7. The best cheese shop is the one near you run by someone who will let you taste everything before buying.
Pairings

What goes with what

Forget the complicated charts. These combinations work every time.

🍯

Honey + Blue

Raw honey on Gorgonzola or Roquefort. The sweetness tames the bite. The single best gateway to blue cheese.

🍐

Pear + Aged Gouda

Caramel notes in the cheese, crisp sweetness in the fruit. A pairing that makes you look like you know things.

🥖

Baguette + Brie

The most French thing you can do. Warm bread, room-temp brie, nothing else. Simplicity as a statement.

🍷

Port + Stilton

The classic English pairing. Sweet fortified wine against crumbly, tangy blue. Works after dinner when you want to feel distinguished.

🍎

Apple + Cheddar

Tart apple, sharp cheddar. The original cheese pairing. There's a reason the English have been doing this for centuries.

🫒

Olive Oil + Pecorino

Drizzle good olive oil over chunks of Pecorino with black pepper. That's basically cacio e pepe without the pasta.
